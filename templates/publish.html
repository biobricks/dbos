Publish an Invention in the Distributed Bulletin of Open Science

Use this form to claim a scientific invention for the public domain by publishing it to the Distributed Bullet of Open Science. For more about DBOS, see the about page.

Do not break the law, any institutional policy, or any contract by publishing in DBOS. Review the brief users guide for notes on legal restrictions that may apply to your work.

Inventor

Either the inventor or someone helping the inventor to use the computer must write the responses to this form, fill it out, and submit it.

To publishing in DBOS, you must grant a public copyright license for your publication. Usually, the author of those materials is the one who has to give the license. The answers to this form, especially the title, description, and any attachments, should be the inventor’s work.

Name

Please provide your full name.

If you want to publish to DBOS anonymously, leave this field blank.

Affiliation

Please provide the legal name of the commercial, academic, nonprofit, governmental, or other organization you work for, if any.

If you want to publish to DBOS anonymously, leave this field blank.

Title

Provide a title for your invention, describing what it is and how it is useful, in the terms most natural for you and colleagues in your field.

There’s no need to “translate” your title into legal jargon, as you might for a patent, or into everyday language, as you might for a press release.

Description

Explain the best way that someone in your field can make and use your invention. Feel free to use multiple paragraphs if necessary.

This is the most important part. If your description enables others in your field to make and use your invention, publishing it creates “prior art” that can stop later patent applications for the invention. If at all possible, have a colleague review your description and tell you if it’s missing any nonobvious details.

Safety Notes

Optionally describe any special safety precautions others might like to take when trying and using your invention, to protect themselves, other people, and the environment.

Attachments

If images or other data files are essential to describing your invention, attach them here. If the description above is enough to teach others how to use what you’ve found, there’s no need for any attachments.

If you can describe your invention with a standard-format file, like a FASTA or SBOL file for genetic information, please attach it. Well-structured data will make finding your submission much easier.

Metadata

“Metadata”, or data about data, help researchers and computer programs catalog, index, and search digital records like your publication. Taking a few seconds to add metadata to your publication transforms it from a needle in a haystack into a useful record for reseachers.

Patent Classifications

The International Patent Classification is a standardized taxonomy of invention classes referred to by codes. For example, C12N 0/900 denotes oxidoreductases. Patent offices and patent researchers the world over use IPC to categorize patents and patent applications, as well as to find prior art.
